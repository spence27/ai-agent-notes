AI & how buyers pick an agent

Working notes · May–June 2026

AI is quietly becoming how buyers pick an agent. I got curious what the AI tools actually say when a buyer asks for one, so I spent a little time testing it: the same questions, asked over and over across three engines, every run logged. A dozen-plus real Colorado agents showed up in the results. Some of it surprised me. A lot of what's being sold around it is junk.

1. The adoption numbers

The surveys here are real ones, and the numbers were bigger than I expected. People don't fully trust it yet. They use it anyway.

  • 82% of active buyers & sellers now use AI for housing research (Realtor.com, Aug 2025)
  • 67% / 54% use ChatGPT / Gemini — the two named leaders (Realtor.com, Aug 2025)
  • 48% of near-term buyers have or will use AI to buy a home (NerdWallet, Nov 2025)
  • 9 of 10 runs where ChatGPT gave a real agent's brokerage wrong (my testing, Jun 2026)

2. What that does to agents

Most agents are invisible to it, or described wrong.

Invisible. Ask ChatGPT for the "best agent in [a town]" and the local top producer often simply isn't in the answer. Someone else is.

Misrepresented. When AI does name an agent, it can get the story wrong — an old brokerage, stale numbers. There's a live example below.

Unmonitored. Nobody's checking. Nothing in a normal agent's stack surfaces any of this — it all happens out of view.

3. This isn't SEO

Being #1 on Google doesn't mean an AI knows you exist.

What it is: getting ChatGPT, Google's AI, and the rest to find an agent, get them right, and recommend them when a buyer asks. All of it rides on signals the AI already trusts. None of it is a hidden trick.

What it isn't: a setting to flip, a file to upload, or classic SEO under a new name. And it isn't a single number — which is a big part of why it's so easy to fake.

4. The engines disagree (the part that surprised me)

There is no single "AI visibility" — the engines flatly disagree. One question, "best real estate agents in Longmont," with no names in it. I ran it 7–10 times on each engine, for two real Colorado agents, scripted so every ask was identical. Here's what came back.

5. Unstable — and wrong

The results are unstable, and AI invents facts.

Ask twice, get two answers. Run the same question back-to-back and the names change. The honest number is a rate — how many runs out of ten an agent shows up in. A "report" built on one screenshot is mostly noise. It drifts over weeks too, not just run to run; any one-time "AI audit" is a photo of the weather.

It makes up your story. In 9 of my 10 ChatGPT runs, it pinned one of those agents to a brokerage she'd already left. Perplexity got it wrong too, with a different wrong answer. AI states things about people that simply aren't true. And the wrong version doesn't live anywhere she controls: it lives in stale directory profiles she's probably forgotten exist. An agent's own website turns out to be one of the last things AI reads — when it can read it at all; a surprising number of agent sites are blank to a crawler.

6. Where each engine gets its answers

The standard advice (reviews, a tended Google Business Profile, one consistent identity across the web) is real, but it's table stakes — it's in every vendor's deck. The part nobody tells you: the engines aren't one thing. When I traced where each one's names were actually coming from, the sources barely overlapped. Each engine has its own diet.

ChatGPT eats lists. Its web search runs on Bing's index, not Google's. And in my runs, nearly every name it served traced back to a handful of "best agent" list sites (Expertise, FastExpert) — low-traffic pages no agent takes seriously, quoted straight into ChatGPT's answers. It's why the 787-deal producer is 4 of 10 there: she's mostly missing from the lists it reads.

Google's AI eats your Google profile. It reads the Business Profile and Zillow/Maps reviews, and it works from current data — it was the only engine that stated the right brokerage, every single time. It's also the only engine an agent can move quickly. Weeks, not quarters.

Perplexity eats one list at a time. It doesn't blend sources. Each run it grabs a single list and reads the names off it: Yelp this run, U.S. News the next, somebody's brokerage page the third. Visibility there is a count of tickets you hold, not a rank. It's also why a single Perplexity screenshot proves nothing.

Underneath all three: Reddit. Local threads from real people asking "who's good in [town]?" keep showing up in the citations. They sit upstream of the answers. Every AI-marketing course says "go post on Reddit," which is advice no working agent will ever follow, and paying to seed fake threads gets accounts banned and doesn't survive the next model update. The version that would actually compound is mechanical: a small watcher that flags a new thread the day it appears and taps the agent (or their assistant) to leave one genuinely useful answer. Two real replies a week, upstream of every engine. I could build that in an evening. Nobody's doing it.

7. The snake oil

This space is already full of confident nonsense. A cottage industry has formed around selling agents "AI optimization." Plenty of what it sells does nothing:

  • llms.txt files
  • "GEO schema" packages
  • mass AI city pages
  • paid Reddit placement
  • "guaranteed ChatGPT placement"
  • "first AI calls in 6 weeks"

The first two get ignored by the very engines they claim to influence.

And from the outside, an agent has almost no way to tell the real thing from the placebo.

Where I've landed

For now: the buyer shift is real, and it can actually be measured — that's the part I didn't expect. The industry forming on top of it mostly sells certainty it doesn't have. Still pulling on this.
